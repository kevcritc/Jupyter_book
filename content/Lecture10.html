11 Notes on Carbon Nanomaterials#

11.1 Introduction to Carbon Nanomaterials#

Nanomaterials are materials that have structural features at the nanometer scale, typically below 100 nm. Carbon nanomaterials exhibit unique mechanical, electrical, and thermal properties, making them essential in modern technology.

11.2 Graphite#

11.2.1 Physical Properties:#

  • Soft and Slippery: Layers of carbon atoms are held together by weak van der Waals forces, allowing them to slide over each other.

  • Grey-Black, Opaque Appearance: It has a metallic sheen but is not reflective like metals.

  • Lightweight: Density of ~2.26 g/cm³.

  • Good Electrical Conductor: Each carbon atom is bonded to three others, leaving a free electron that moves through the layers.

  • High Melting Point (~3,600°C): Strong covalent bonds within layers require significant energy to break.

  • Insoluble in Water and Organic Solvents: Strong covalent bonds prevent dissolution.

11.2.2 Key Equations:#

  • Electrical Conductivity:

    \(\sigma = \frac{1}{\rho}\)

    where \( \rho \) is the electrical resistivity.

  • Thermal Conductivity:

    \(k = \frac{1}{3} C_v v l\)

    where \( C_v \) is the heat capacity, \( v\) is the speed of sound in the material, and \( l \) is the mean free path of phonons.

11.2.3 Example Calculation:#

Estimate the thermal conductivity of graphite given: \( C_v = 2.0 \times 10^6 \) J/m³K,\( v = 2000 \) m/s, and \( l = 10^{-7} \) m.

Solution:

\(k = \frac{1}{3} (2.0 \times 10^6) (2000) (10^{-7})\)

\(k = \frac{1}{3} (4.0 \times 10^5)\)

\(k = 1.33 \times 10^5 \text{ W/mK}\)

12.3 Diamond#

12.3.1 Physical Properties:#

  • Extremely Hard: Strong covalent bonding in a tetrahedral structure makes it the hardest natural material.

  • Transparent and Colourless: Impurities can add color (e.g., nitrogen makes it yellow, boron makes it blue).

  • High Density (~3.51 g/cm³).

  • Poor Electrical Conductor: All four valence electrons of carbon are bonded, leaving no free electrons.

  • Excellent Thermal Conductor: Better than metals like copper.

12.3.2 Key Equations:#

  • Young’s Modulus:

    \(E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}\)

    where \( \sigma \) is the applied stress and \( \varepsilon \) is the strain.

12.4 Carbon Nanotubes (CNTs)#

12.4.1 Key Equations:#

  • Electrical Conductivity:

    \(\sigma = \frac{q n \mu}{m}\)

where \( q \) is the charge, \( n \) is the carrier density, \( \mu \) is the mobility, and \( m \) is the effective mass.

  • Thermal Conductivity:

\(k = \frac{C_p v l}{3}\)

12.4.2 Example Calculation:#

Find the electrical conductivity of a CNT if charge \( q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \) C, carrier density \( n = 10^{28} \) electrons/m³, mobility \( \mu = 0.1 \) m²/Vs, and effective mass \( m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \) kg.

Solution:

\(\sigma = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19}) (10^{28}) (0.1)}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}\)

\(\sigma = \frac{1.6 \times 10^8}{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}\)

\(\sigma = 1.76 \times 10^7 \text{ S/m}\)

12.5. Graphene#

12.5.1 Physical Properties:#

  • Two-Dimensional Structure: A single layer of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice.

  • High Electrical Conductivity: Due to delocalized π-electrons that move freely.

  • High Mechanical Strength: Young’s modulus of ~1 TPa, higher than steel.

  • High Thermal Conductivity: ~5000 W/mK, among the highest of known materials.

  • Optical Transparency: Absorbs ~2.3% of incident light, making it useful in transparent electronic devices.

12.5.2 Applications:#

  • Nanoelectronics: Graphene’s high electrical conductivity and exceptional charge carrier mobility (~200,000 cm²/V·s) enable ultra-fast transistors and sensors. It is used in field-effect transistors (FETs) for high-performance computing and quantum devices.

  • Flexible Electronics: Due to its thinness and mechanical strength, graphene is employed in flexible displays, foldable smartphones, and wearable technology, maintaining conductivity even under deformation.

  • Energy Storage: Graphene-based supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries offer high charge storage, rapid charge-discharge cycles, and enhanced lifespan due to its large surface area (~2630 m²/g).

  • Biomedical Applications: Graphene’s biocompatibility, high surface area, and antibacterial properties make it suitable for biosensors, targeted drug delivery, and neural interfaces. Its exceptional electrical properties enhance electrode performance in medical devices.

  • Water Filtration: Graphene oxide membranes selectively filter out salts and contaminants due to their atomic-scale precision, enabling advanced desalination and purification technologies.

  • Heat Dissipation: Graphene’s ultrahigh thermal conductivity (~5000 W/mK) is leveraged in cooling solutions for microprocessors, LEDs, and high-power electronics, preventing overheating and enhancing performance.

  • Composites & Coatings: Graphene is added to polymers, metals, and ceramics to enhance mechanical strength, electrical conductivity, and thermal stability, making it useful in aerospace, automotive, and construction materials.

  • Optoelectronics: Graphene’s ability to absorb and manipulate light enables applications in photodetectors, transparent conductive coatings for touchscreens, and next-generation optical communication devices.

  • Nanoelectronics: Used in transistors and circuits due to its high carrier mobility.

  • Flexible Electronics: Ideal for wearable technology and foldable displays.

  • Energy Storage: Applied in supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries for improved energy efficiency.

  • Biomedical Applications: Used in biosensors, drug delivery, and antibacterial coatings.

  • Water Filtration: Graphene membranes can efficiently remove contaminants and desalinate seawater.

12.5.3 Key Equations:#

  • Conductivity of Graphene:

    \(\sigma = \frac{e^2}{h}\)

    where \( e \) is the electron charge and \( h \) is Planck’s constant.

12.5.4 Example Calculation:#

Find the conductivity of graphene using \( e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19} \) C and \( h = 6.63 \times 10^{-34} \) J⋅s.

Solution:

\(\sigma = \frac{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})^2}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}\)

\(\sigma = \frac{2.56 \times 10^{-38}}{6.63 \times 10^{-34}}\)

\(\sigma = 3.86 \times 10^{-5} \text{ S}\)
