14 Semiconductor Materials and Quantum Dots#

14.1 Direct vs. Indirect Band Gap#

  • Direct Band Gap: Electrons transition directly from the conduction band to the valence band, emitting a photon (efficient for optoelectronic applications).

  • Indirect Band Gap: A phonon is required to facilitate the transition, making photon emission less efficient.

14.2 Semiconductor Elements and Their Properties#

Semiconductors are typically made from elements in group IV of the periodic table, as well as from compounds involving elements from groups III-V and II-VI. These materials have moderate band gaps and can be engineered for specific electronic and optical properties.

14.4 Effective Mass of Charge Carriers#

  • The steepness of the E vs. k curve determines the effective mass.

  • The effective mass (\( m^* \)) of a charge carrier is given by: $\( m^* = \hbar^2 \left( \frac{d^2E}{dk^2} \right)^{-1} \)$ where:

    • \( \hbar \) is the reduced Planck’s constant.

    • \( k \) is the wavevector, related to momentum by \( p = \hbar k \).

    • \( E \) is the energy of the charge carrier.

14.5 Quantum Dot Synthesis and Surface Chemistry#

14.5.1 Bottom-Up Synthesis Methods#

Quantum dots (QDs) are typically synthesized using colloidal chemical methods, ensuring control over size, shape, and surface properties. Key synthesis methods include:

  • Hot Injection Synthesis: Rapid precursor injection into a hot solvent, allowing controlled nucleation and growth.

  • Hydrothermal and Solvothermal Synthesis: High-temperature reactions in aqueous or organic solvents.

  • Microemulsion Techniques: Surfactant-stabilized nanodroplets help control QD size.

14.5.2 Surface Chemistry of Quantum Dots#

  • Surface coatings control QD stability, dispersibility, and functionalization.

  • Functional groups influence chemical reactivity and biocompatibility:

    • Carboxyl (-COOH)

    • Amine (-NH₂)

    • Hydroxyl (-OCH₂CH₂OH)

    • Biotin (-CH₃)

    • Thiols (-SH)

  • Importance of Surface Coating:

    • Prevents agglomeration (colloidal stability).

    • Enhances biocompatibility for medical applications.

    • Enables functionalization for biosensing and imaging.

14.6 Applications of Quantum Dots#

14.6.1 Bioimaging and Medical Applications#

  • Fluorescent Labeling: QDs conjugated with biomolecules for cellular tracking.

  • Deep-Tissue Imaging: Near-infrared (NIR) QDs enable deeper penetration into tissues.

  • Cancer Detection: QDs are used to detect and highlight tumor cells.

  • Drug Delivery Tracking: Monitoring the movement of therapeutic drugs within the body.

14.6.2 Quantum Dot-Based Sensors#

  • Glucose Detection: QDs functionalized with glucose oxidase (GOx) for fluorescence-based glucose sensing.

  • pH and Ion Sensing: QDs respond to environmental changes, allowing highly selective chemical detection.

  • Heavy Metal Detection: Functionalized QDs detect toxic metals like lead and mercury.

14.6.3 Electroluminescent Quantum Dot Devices#

14.6.3.1 How Electroluminescence Works#

  • Electroluminescence (EL) occurs when electrical energy is directly converted into light.

  • Charge injection and recombination in QDs result in light emission.

  • Emission color depends on quantum confinement, where smaller QDs emit shorter wavelengths.

  • Electroluminescent QD Devices:

  • Similar to OLED technology but with QDs.

14.6.3.2 Structure of a QD-LED#

  • Anode (ITO-coated glass) → Injects holes.

  • Hole Transport Layer (HTL) → Facilitates hole movement.

  • QD Emissive Layer → Recombination of electrons and holes produces light.

  • Electron Transport Layer (ETL) → Guides electrons into the QD layer.

  • Cathode (Metal layer) → Injects electrons.

14.6.4 Quantum Dot Colour Conversion Technology in Displays#

  • Quantum Dot Light-Emitting Diodes (QD-LEDs):

    • Used in display technologies (e.g., QD-TVs, monitors, and smartphone screens).

    • Mechanism:

      • Blue backlight provides illumination.

      • Red and Green Quantum Dots (QDs) convert part of the light into required colors.

      • Liquid crystals switch pixels on/off using cross-polarizers.

    • Benefits:

      • Improved color accuracy (wide color gamut).

      • Energy-efficient compared to traditional displays.

    • Used in photodetectors, transistors, and chemical sensors.

14.6.5 Other QD Applications:#

  • Modified LEDs → More efficient lighting.

  • Solar Cells → QDs enhance absorption across multiple wavelengths.

  • Quantum Computing → QDs used as qubits.

  • Photocatalysts → Aid in chemical reactions.

  • Temperature sensors → QDs change optical properties with temperature shifts.

14.7 Example Questions and Solutions#

Problem 1: Quantum Dot Emission Wavelength#

A QD with a 2 eV band gap is excited by 400 nm light. What is the emitted wavelength?

Solution:

  1. Emitted energy: \(E_{emit} = 2.0 eV\)

  2. Find emitted wavelength: \(\lambda = \frac{hc}{E_{emit}} = 620 nm\) Answer: 620 nm (Red region).

Problem 2: Semiconductor Conductivity#

A silicon semiconductor has an electron mobility of 1350 cm²/Vs and a hole mobility of 480 cm²/Vs. If the electron carrier density is 10¹⁶ cm⁻³ and the hole carrier density is 10¹⁵ cm⁻³, find the conductivity.

Solution:

  1. Convert units if needed (S/m, m²/Vs, etc.).

  2. Use the conductivity equation: \( \sigma = q (n \mu_e + p \mu_h) \) \( \sigma = (1.602 \times 10^{-19}) [(10^{16} \times 1350) + (10^{15} \times 480)] \)

  3. Solve for \( \sigma \): \( \sigma = (1.602 \times 10^{-19}) (1.35 \times 10^{19} + 4.8 \times 10^{17}) \) \( \sigma = (1.602 \times 10^{-19}) (1.398 \times 10^{19}) \) \( \sigma = 2.24 S/m \) Final Answer: \( \sigma = 2.24 S/m \)

14.8 Summary & Key Takeaways#

  • Quantum confinement leads to size-dependent band gaps.

  • Quantum dots are synthesized using chemical methods and can be engineered for different properties.

  • Surface chemistry plays a crucial role in stability, biocompatibility, and functionalization.

  • Quantum dots are widely used in bioimaging, biosensing, chemical detection, and energy applications.

  • Electroluminescent devices use quantum dots for high-efficiency lighting and displays.

  • Worked examples help reinforce calculations for quantum dot emissions and semiconductor conductivity.
