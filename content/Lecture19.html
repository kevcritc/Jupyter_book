19.1 Tumour Growth and Nanoparticles#

A tumour is an abnormal mass of tissue that arises when cells divide and grow uncontrollably. Tumours can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). Malignant tumours can invade nearby tissues and spread (metastasise) to other parts of the body through the bloodstream or lymphatic system.

19.2 How Do Nanoparticles Target Cancer?#

Nanoparticles can reach and act on tumours using two strategies:

19.2.1 Passive Targeting#

  • Tumours have leaky blood vessels and poor lymphatic drainage.

  • Nanoparticles accumulate in tumours due to the Enhanced Permeation and Retention (EPR) effect.

  • No specific targeting involved ‚Äì relies on tumour environment.

19.2.2 Active Targeting#

  • Tumour cells overexpress specific receptors or transporters.

  • Nanoparticles are decorated with ligands that bind to these receptors.

  • The cell ‚Äúthinks‚Äù it‚Äôs internalising something useful and brings the nanoparticle inside.

üß™ Active targeting requires chemical modification of the nanoparticle surface.

19.3 Benefits of Using Nanoparticles for Cancer Therapy#

19.3.1 Improved Drug Properties#

  • Many anti-cancer drugs are:

    • Hydrophobic (poorly soluble in water)

    • Unstable (degraded quickly in blood or excreted)

  • Nanoparticles:

    • Increase drug solubility

    • Prolong circulation time

19.3.2 Reduced Side Effects#

  • More targeted drug delivery ‚Üí fewer drugs affect healthy cells.

  • Requires lower doses for effectiveness.

19.3.3 Better Drug Delivery#

  • Nanoparticles can:

    • Cross biological barriers (e.g., blood-brain barrier, skin)

    • Deliver drugs to specific tissues or cells

    • Accumulate selectively in diseased organs

19.3.4 Controlled Drug Release#

  • Nanoparticles can be engineered to:

    • Release drugs slowly over time (sustained release)

    • Release drugs in response to stimuli:

      • pH

      • Temperature

      • Enzymes

      • Light

19.3.5 Enhanced Diagnostics and Imaging#

  • Nanoparticles allow early and sensitive disease detection

    • E.g. Lab-on-a-chip, single-cell analysis, bioMEMs

  • Act as contrast agents in:

    • MRI

    • CT scans

    • Ultrasound

19.4 Theranostic Nanoparticles#

üìò ‚ÄúTheranostics‚Äù = Therapy + Diagnostics

  • Multifunctional nanoparticles:

    • Deliver drugs and

    • Track drug location and effectiveness

  • Combines:

    • Drug delivery

    • Imaging

    • Stimuli-responsive release

    • Active targeting

    • Diagnostics

  • Some nanoparticles are coated with drugs + fluorescent dyes or metals (e.g., gold or iron oxide) for dual purpose use.

19.5 Safety & Toxicology#

19.5.1 Biological Risks:#

  • Nanoparticles have:

    • Small size

    • High surface area

    • High chemical reactivity

  • Can cause:

    • Toxicity

    • Immune reactions

    • Accumulation in organs

    • Unexpected side effects

Thorough preclinical testing is essential before human trials.

19.5.2 Preclinical Evaluation#

19.5.2.1 In Vitro (‚Äúin glass‚Äù):#

  • Performed in test tubes or petri dishes

  • Useful for early screening

19.5.2.2 In Vivo (‚Äúin life‚Äù):#

  • Performed in living organisms (e.g. mice)

  • Provides information on:

    • Absorption

    • Distribution

    • Metabolism

    • Excretion

    • Toxicity

19.6 Ethical & Social Issues#

19.6.1 Sustainability and Environment#

  • Need guidelines for:

    • Occupational exposure (lab safety)

    • Environmental impact (waste disposal)

  • Long-term effects in ecosystems are not well understood

19.6.2 Regulation and Policy#

  • No one-size-fits-all: Every nanomaterial is different.

  • Regulatory bodies:

    • EMA ‚Äì European Medicines Agency

    • FDA ‚Äì U.S. Food and Drug Administration

    • ISO, CEN ‚Äì Standards organizations

    • EPA ‚Äì U.S. Environmental Protection Agency

19.6.3 Socioeconomic Considerations#

  • Cost, access, and public perception influence how nanomedicine is adopted.

  • Need transparent, inclusive dialogue between scientists, regulators, and the public.

19.7 Summary#

  • Nanoparticles improve cancer treatment by enhancing delivery, reducing side effects, and enabling imaging.

  • Theranostic platforms combine therapy and diagnosis in a single nanostructure.

  • Safety and ethics must be considered, including rigorous preclinical testing and public policy development.
