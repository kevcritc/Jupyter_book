20 Seeing the Nano World ‚Äì Quick Introduction into Imaging Techniques in Nanotechnology#

Understanding the structure and function of materials at the nanoscale requires powerful imaging techniques that go far beyond conventional light microscopy.

20.1 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)#

  • SEM uses a focused beam of electrons to scan the surface of a sample.

  • Electrons interact with the atoms on the surface, generating signals:

    • Secondary electrons ‚Üí show surface texture

    • Backscattered electrons ‚Üí show contrast due to atomic number

  • Resolution: Down to nanometre scale

‚úÖ SEM is excellent for viewing surface structures of nanomaterials in 3D-like detail.

20.2 Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)#

  • TEM sends electrons through an ultra-thin sample.

  • Image is formed based on how electrons are transmitted or scattered.

  • Very high resolution ‚Äî can see individual atoms in some cases.

‚úÖ TEM is ideal for studying internal structures at the atomic level.

20.3 Scanning Tunneling Microscopy (STM)#

20.3.1 Principle:#

  • A sharp metal tip is brought close to a conducting surface.

  • A voltage is applied between tip and sample.

  • Quantum tunneling of electrons occurs:

    • When the tip is near the surface, electrons can ‚Äútunnel‚Äù through the vacuum gap.

    • Tunneling current increases as tip gets closer.

20.3.2 Imaging:#

  • If current is kept constant, the tip moves up and down to maintain it.

  • This motion is used to map the surface with atomic resolution.

üí° STM was the first technique to directly image individual atoms on surfaces.

20.4 Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM)#

20.4.1 How it works:#

  • A cantilever with a sharp tip is brought near the sample surface.

  • Forces between the tip and surface (van der Waals, electrostatic, etc.) cause the cantilever to deflect.

  • A laser beam tracks the deflection.

20.4.2 Hooke‚Äôs Law:#

\[F = kx\]

  • F: Force

  • k: Spring constant of cantilever

  • x: Deflection

20.4.3 Advantages:#

  • Works on non-conductive materials

  • Can image soft biological samples (e.g. DNA, proteins)

  • Can image in aqueous environment

20.5 Fluorescence Microscopy#

  • Used to image specific molecules in biological samples.

  • Sample is treated with fluorescent dyes or tagged proteins.

  • When excited by light of one wavelength, fluorophores emit light at another wavelength.

‚úÖ Useful for live-cell imaging, tracking molecules, and bio-labeling.
