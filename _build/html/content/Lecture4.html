1. Referencing and Citations in Essays#

1.1 In-Text Citations#

  • Use a numerical system in square brackets, e.g., [1].

  • Place the number before the full stop.

  • Example: ‚ÄúLight scattering in films is well understood [1].‚Äù

1.2 Creating a Reference List#

  • List references in order of appearance.

  • Format:

    [Number] Author(s), Title, Journal/Book, Volume, Pages, Year.

  • Example:

    [1] A. Smith, "Optical Properties of Thin Films," *J. Optics*, vol. 45, pp. 23-30, 2020.

1.3 Managing References#

  • Reference management tools: EndNote, Zotero, Mendeley.

  • In LaTeX: Use BibTeX with a numbered style (e.g., IEEEtran).

  • In Word: Use the built-in citation manager under the ‚ÄúReferences‚Äù tab.

1.4 Acceptable Reference Styles#

  • The preferred style is numerical referencing (1-3 format):

    1. Journal articles: Author(s), Journal, Year, Volume, Pages.

    2. Books: Author(s), Title, Edition, Year, Publisher, Pages.

    3. Websites: Author(s), Access Date.

    4. Lecture notes: Author(s), Year, Date.

1.5 What Makes a Good Reference?#

  • A primary source of information.

  • Accurate and reputable.

  • Accessible to readers.

  • Relevant to the topic.

  • Contains all necessary details for verification.

  • Consistent formatting.

2. Importance of Physics Scaling Laws in Nanotechnology#

2.1 Why Size Matters#

  • At nanoscale, surface effects dominate over bulk properties.

  • Example: Nanoparticles have lower melting points than bulk materials.

2.2 Scaling of Forces#

  • Gravity becomes negligible.

  • Electromagnetic and surface tension forces dominate.

  • Example: Van der Waals forces cause water droplets to stick to surfaces.

2.3 Quantum Effects#

  • Classical mechanics fails at the nanoscale.

  • Quantum mechanics determines behavior.

  • Example: Quantum dots have discrete electron energy levels.

2.4 Applications in Technology#

  • Scaling laws help in designing nanoscale devices.

  • Examples:

    • Miniaturization in microelectronics (e.g., transistors).

    • Nanomedicine (e.g., drug delivery, sensors).

3. Scaling in Physics#

There is a general method for solving scaling problems. These basic steps can be applied generally and course if your maths is a strong you may see that you can take some short cuts!

  1. Identify or Derive the Governing Equation

    • Determine the fundamental equation that describes the system.

  2. Identify Length-Scale Terms

    • Locate terms in the equation that involve a length scale.

    • If necessary, use material property equations (e.g., density, conductivity, capacitance) to introduce length-dependent terms.

  3. Rearrange the Equation

    • Express the equation in a form where the target property (e.g., speed, force, time) is the subject.

  4. Convert to a Proportionality Expression

    • Replace the equals sign with ‚Äúproportional to‚Äù (‚àù).

    • Ensure the equation contains only the target term and length scale terms (L‚Äôs).

    • Set all other dimensionless constants to unity.

  5. Determine the Scaling Exponent

    • Rewrite the expression in the form \(L^n\).

    • Identify the value of \(n\) (e.g., 1, 2, -1/2) based on the proportionality relation.

  6. Write Down Known Values for the Given Scale

    • Example: If the system is a boat, assign values such as \(L_1 = 2\) (length) and \(v_1 = 10\) (speed).

  7. Write Down Known Values for the Target Scale

    • Example: If scaling the boat, assign \(L_2 = \text{new length}\).

  8. Set Up the Scaling Equation

\[ \frac{v_1}{v_2} = \left( \frac{L_1}{L_2} \right)^n \]

  1. Solve for the Unknown Term

    • Rearrange the equation to express the unknown in terms of the known values:

\[v_2 = v_1 \times \left( \frac{L_2}{L_1} \right)^n\]

  1. Substitute Values and Compute the Answer

    • Insert numerical values and calculate the final result.

3.1 Electric Field#

  • Electric field strength is inversely proportional to characteristic size \( L \).

3.2 Capacitance#

  • Capacitance increases with characteristic size \( L \).

3.3 Inductance#

  • Inductance is proportional to characteristic size \( L \).

3.4 Heat Transfer Scaling#

  • Energy needed to heat a material scales with mass \( L^3 \).

  • Heat flux follows Newton‚Äôs Cooling Law.

3.5 Scaling in Optics#

  • Limits feature size in conventional lithography.

3.6 Biological Scaling Laws#

Biological systems follow specific scaling laws that impact metabolism, strength, and lifespan as size changes.

3.6.1 Metabolic Scaling (Kleiber‚Äôs Law)#

  • Metabolic rate (energy consumption per unit time) scales with body mass \(M\) as:

    \( \text{Metabolic Rate} \propto M^{3/4} \) \(=\propto L^{9/4} \)

  • This means larger animals consume energy more efficiently per unit mass.

    • Example: Elephants have a lower metabolic rate per gram of tissue than mice.

    • Smaller animals require higher food intake per gram of body weight.

Figure 1. Plot of Metabolic Rate versus mass for a few example animals.

3.6.2 Strength and Bone Scaling**#

  • Strength depends on cross-sectional area **(muscle/bone strength \( S \propto L^2 \) **, while **weight scales with volume \( W \propto L^3 \) **.

  • Larger animals are proportionally weaker:

    • Example: An ant can carry many times its body weight, but an elephant cannot.

    • Bone thickness must increase disproportionately for large animals to support their weight.

3.6.3 Heart Rate and Lifespan**#

  • Heart rate scales as:

    \( \text{Heart Rate} \propto L^{-1/4} \)

Larger animals have slower heart rates and longer lifespans.

  • Example, Mice (500 bpm, 2-year lifespan) vs. Elephants (30 bpm, 60+ years).

  • Across species, most mammals have about a billion heartbeats per lifetime.

3.6.4 Effects of Scaling on Strength#

  • Smaller animals: High strength-to-weight ratio (e.g., ants can carry many times their weight).

  • Larger animals: Need thicker muscles (e.g., elephants).

  • Scaling humans up:

    • If doubled in height:

      • Strength increases by 4 times (remember proportional to surface area).

      • Body mass increases by 8 times (why - proportional to mass).

      • The person would struggle to stand.

4. Moore‚Äôs Law#

  • Number of transistors doubles every 2 years.

  • Transistor size must shrink to avoid doubling the chip‚Äôs surface area.

  • Every two years:

    • Area halves \( L^2 \).

    • Characteristic length \( L \) decreases by \(\sqrt{2}\).

5. Writing a Technical Report#

5.1 Style Guidelines#

  • Target audience: Technical magazine.

  • Use formal language:

    • Avoid contractions (e.g., ‚Äúdon‚Äôt‚Äù ‚Üí ‚Äúdo not‚Äù).

    • Avoid clich√©s.

  • Be concise: Remove unnecessary words.

  • Reference facts that are not common knowledge.

  • Use subheadings for clarity.

  • Low detail in introduction/conclusion, high detail in core sections.

  • Avoid personal opinions/emotions.

6. Nanophysics - The Atom#

6.1 Discovery of Atomic Spectra#

  • 1800s: Spectroscopic studies revealed distinct emission lines for different gases.

  • Example: Hydrogen, Helium, Sodium, Potassium.

Figure 4. The Hydrogen spectral lines in the visible spectrum.

6.2 The Rydberg Formula#

  • General formula for hydrogen spectral lines:

\(\frac{1}{\lambda}=R\left(\frac{1}{n_1^2} -\frac{1}{n_2^2}\right)\)

where, \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) are \(>0\), \(n_2>n_1\), and \(R = 1.097776*10^7 \text{m}^{-1}\)

6.3 Classical View of the Atom#

  • Coulomb‚Äôs Law governs attraction between electrons and nucleus.

  • Circular motion of electrons leads to centripetal force.

Figure 5. The Classical model of the atom with the forces added.

Coulomb‚Äôs law is the starting point with the nuclear charge \(Ze\) and charge of the electron \(e\). \begin{equation} F=\frac{kZe^2}{r^2} \tag{1} \end{equation}

This force is equal the centripetal force \begin{equation} F=\frac{mv^2}{r} \tag{2} \end{equation}

where \(v\) is the speed of the electron, \(m\) is the mass, and \(r\) is the distance from the centre of the atom. Equating 1 and 2 and simplifying gives,

\begin{equation} mv^2=\frac{kZe^2}{r} \tag{3} \end{equation}

Note that by multipling by $\frac{1}{2} we arrive at an expression for the kinetic energy (K). \begin{equation} \frac{1}{2}mv^2=\frac{kZe^2}{2r}\tag{4} \end{equation}

6.4 Energy of the Electron#

  • Potential Energy (PE): Work done moving the electron to distance r . \begin{equation} U=\frac{-kZe^2}{r}\tag{5} \end{equation}

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): Motion of electron around nucleus. Remember from Eq. 4 we have: \begin{equation} K=\frac{1}{2}mv^2=\frac{kZe^2}{2r}\tag{6} \end{equation}

  • Total Energy (E):

\begin{equation} E=U+K \tag{7} \end{equation}

Sub in Eq. 5 and Eq. 6 into 7 to get the total energy, E

\begin{equation} E=-\frac{1}{2}\frac{kZe^2}{r} \tag{8} \end{equation}

  • Classical mechanics predicts electron should spiral into nucleus, which does not happen.

  • This problem led to the development of quantum mechanics.

Key Takeaways#

  • Scaling laws explain why nanoscale materials behave differently.

  • Quantum mechanics is essential for nanotechnology.

  • Moore‚Äôs Law predicts the continued miniaturization of electronics.

  • Nanophysics underpins advancements in medicine, materials, and technology.

  • Proper referencing is crucial for academic writing.
