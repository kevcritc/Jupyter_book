Carbon Materials#

1. Hybridization and sp Orbitals#

  • Hybridization occurs when atomic orbitals mix to form new, equivalent hybrid orbitals.

  • sp Hybridization:

    • The 2s orbital mixes with just one of the 2p orbitals to form two equivalent sp orbitals.

    • The two sp orbitals are arranged linearly, with the remaining 2p orbitals perpendicular to them.

1.1 sp¬π Hybridized Carbon & Bonding in Ethyne (C‚ÇÇH‚ÇÇ)#

  • In ethyne (acetylene), each carbon is sp hybridized, leading to:

    1. Two C-H œÉ bonds:

      \[C(sp^1) - H(1s)\]

    2. One œÉ bond between the two carbons:

      \[C(sp^1) - C(sp^1)\]

    3. Two œÄ bonds (overlapping p orbitals), creating a triple bond (C‚â°C):

      \[C \equiv C\]

2. Conjugation and Delocalisation#

  • Conjugation occurs when double and single bonds alternate, allowing p orbitals to overlap, forming a delocalized œÄ electron cloud.

  • Effects of Conjugation:

    • Leads to characteristic spectral absorptions (UV-visible).

    • As conjugation increases, the energy difference between bonding and anti-bonding œÄ orbitals decreases, causing absorption in the visible range.

3. Carbon, Nitrogen, and Oxygen#

  • Electron configurations:

    • Carbon:

      \[1s^2 2s^2 2p^2 \]

    • Nitrogen:

      \[1s^2 2s^2 2p^3\]

    • Oxygen:

      \[1s^2 2s^2 2p^4\]

  • Lone pairs:

    • Nitrogen and oxygen have unbonded electron pairs (lone pairs), which affect molecular shape.

    • Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonded electrons, causing deviations from perfect tetrahedral angles.

    • Example:

      • Methane (CH‚ÇÑ): Perfect tetrahedral.

      • Ammonia (NH‚ÇÉ): Slightly compressed due to lone pair repulsion.

      • Water (H‚ÇÇO): More compressed, with a bond angle of 104.4¬∞.

4. Hydrogen Bonding & Van der Waals Forces#

4.1 Hydrogen Bonds in Water#

  • Hydrogen bonds occur due to strong dipole-dipole interactions.

  • Water‚Äôs hydrogen bonds result in:

    • High boiling point.

    • Ice having a lower density than water due to an open hexagonal structure.

4.2 Van der Waals Interactions#

  • Van der Waals forces arise from fluctuating dipoles that create weak attractive interactions.

  • Factors influencing strength:

    • Size of electron cloud (larger atoms/molecules have stronger Van der Waals forces).

    • Proximity of molecules.

4.3 Lennard-Jones Potential (LJ Potential)#

  • Describes Van der Waals interactions mathematically:

    • Attractive term (dominant at longer distances).

    • Repulsive term (dominant at short distances, due to the Pauli exclusion principle).

    • The Lennard-Jones potential equation:

      \[U(r) = 4\epsilon \left[ \left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12} - \left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^6 \right]\]

      where:

      • \(\epsilon \) = depth of the potential well (interaction strength).

      • \( \sigma \) = atomic radius.

      • \( r \) = interatomic separation.

5. Carbon Allotropes#

  • Carbon exists in different structural forms due to its ability to form multiple bonding types.

5.1 Graphite#

  • Structure:

    • Layers of carbon atoms arranged in hexagonal sheets.

    • Each carbon is sp¬≤ hybridized, forming œÉ and œÄ bonds.

    • Weak interlayer forces allow easy sliding between layers.

  • Properties:

    • Good electrical conductor (due to delocalized electrons).

    • Good conductor of heat

    • High thermal stability.

    • Self-lubricating (low friction).

  • Applications:

    • Pencils, electrodes, brake drums.

5.2 Diamond#

  • Structure:

    • Each carbon forms four œÉ bonds, creating a 3D tetrahedral network.

    • Strong covalent bonds make it extremely hard.

  • Properties:

    • Transparent and colorless.

    • Hardest naturally occurring material.

    • Electrical insulator but excellent heat conductor.

  • Applications:

    • Cutting tools, jewelry, drill tips.
