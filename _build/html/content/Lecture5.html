Lecture Notes: The Bohr Model and Quantum Mechanics#

1. Introduction to the Bohr Model#

  • The Bohr model was developed as an improvement over classical physics to explain atomic structure, particularly the hydrogen atom.

  • It successfully explained the discrete spectral lines of hydrogen.

  • Bohr was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1922 for this work.

2. The Bohr Postulates#

2.1 First Postulate: Stationary States#

  • Electrons in an atom can only occupy certain stable, non-radiating circular orbits known as stationary states.

  • These orbits are quantized, meaning electrons cannot exist in between these orbits.

2.2 Second Postulate: Energy and Radiation#

  • The frequency of radiation emitted or absorbed by an electron is related to the energy difference between two stationary states:

\begin{equation} E_f - E_i = h\nu \tag{1} \end{equation}

where:

  • \(E_f\) and \(E_i\) are the final and initial energy levels.

  • \(h\) is Planck‚Äôs constant.

  • \(\nu\) is the frequency of the emitted radiation.

2.3 Third Postulate: Quantized Angular Momentum#

  • The allowed electron orbits are determined by the condition that the electron‚Äôs angular momentum is quantized: \begin{equation} m v r = n \hbar \tag{2} \end{equation}

    where:

    • \(m\) is the electron‚Äôs mass.

    • \(v\) is the velocity of the electron.

    • \(r\) is the radius of the orbit.

    • \(n\) is a positive integer \( n = 1, 2, 3, ... \)

    • \(\hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi}\) is the reduced Planck‚Äôs constant.

  • This explains why only certain orbits are allowed and why electrons do not spiral into the nucleus.

3. The Bohr Radius#

  • The radius of the lowest energy orbit (( n=1 )) is known as the Bohr radius:

\begin{equation} a_0 = \frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2}{m e^2} \tag{3} \end{equation}

  • For hydrogen Z=1 , the Bohr radius is:

\begin{equation} a_0 = 0.0529 \text{nm} \tag{4} \end{equation}

  • This result was derived using classical mechanics and electrostatic force principles.

4. Energy Levels in the Bohr Model#

  • The quantized energy levels for hydrogen are given by:

\begin{equation} E_n = -\frac{E_0Z^2}{n^2} \tag{5} \end{equation}

  • Key values:

    • \(E_0 = 13.6 \text{eV}\)

    • Ground state n=1: \(E_1 = -E_0 \) eV

    • Excited states: \(E_2 = -3.4\) eV etc.

    • Ionization limit n = ‚àû: \(E = 0\) eV

  • The energy difference between levels determines the wavelength of emitted light:

\begin{equation} \lambda = \frac{1240 \text{ eV‚ãÖnm}}{E} \tag{6} \end{equation}

  • This successfully explained the Rydberg formula for hydrogen‚Äôs spectral lines.

5. The Bohr Model and Spectral Series#

  • The Bohr model explains the emission spectra of hydrogen:

    • Lyman series: Transitions to \( n=1 \) (UV region)

    • Balmer series: Transitions to \( n=2 \) (Visible region)

    • Paschen series: Transitions to \( n=3 \) (Infrared region)

6. Limitations of the Bohr Model#

While the Bohr model was a significant advancement, it had several limitations:

  • No justification for stationary orbits or quantized angular momentum.

  • Failed to accurately predict spectra for atoms other than hydrogen.

  • Could not explain fine structure (small spectral line splitting).

7. Wave-Particle Duality and Quantum Mechanics#

7.1 The Wave-Particle Duality#

  • Matter and light exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties.

  • Each particle has an associated de Broglie wavelength:

\begin{equation} \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} \tag{7} \end{equation}

  • This led to the concept of quantized electron waves around the nucleus.

7.2 Quantum Mechanics and Schr√∂dinger‚Äôs Equation#

  • The Bohr model was an important step in understanding atomic structure, but modern quantum mechanics provides a more complete picture.

  • Instead of treating electrons as orbiting particles, quantum mechanics describes them as waves using a function called the wavefunction \( \Psi(x,t) \), which contains all the information about the electron.

The Schr√∂dinger Equation#

The fundamental equation of quantum mechanics is the Schr√∂dinger equation, which determines how the wavefunction \(\Psi\) behaves.

Time-Independent Schr√∂dinger Equation#

For an electron moving in an atom, we use the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation:

\begin{equation} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\Psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\Psi = E\Psi \tag{8} \end{equation}

where:

  • \(\hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi} \) is the reduced Planck‚Äôs constant.

  • \( m \) is the mass of the electron.

  • \( V(x) \) is the potential energy (for a hydrogen atom, this is the electrostatic attraction to the nucleus).

  • \( E \) is the total energy of the electron.

  • \( \Psi(x) \) is the wavefunction, which tells us where the electron is most likely to be found.

In Three Dimensions (Spherical Form)#

Since atoms are three-dimensional, we use spherical coordinates:

\begin{equation} \frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 \Psi + V(r) \Psi = E \Psi \tag{9} \end{equation}

where:

  • \( \nabla^2 \) is the Laplacian operator, which accounts for the three-dimensional motion of the electron.

  • \( r \) is the distance from the nucleus.

  • \( V(r) = -\frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r} \) is the Coulomb potential energy from the nucleus.

8. Conclusion#

  • The Bohr model was a major step toward understanding atomic structure.

  • It successfully explained hydrogen‚Äôs spectral lines but failed for more complex atoms.

  • Wave-particle duality and quantum mechanics later refined our understanding of atomic behavior.
